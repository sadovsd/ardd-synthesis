# Jan Hoeijmakers at ARDD — DNA repair, the growth–maintenance trade-off, and aging

Talk: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rVE77v6YfmY

## Concepts

- Repair of DNA is super linked with aging.
- **The key biological trade-off**: a cell/body can't maximally do both "grow" and "protect/maintain" at the same time. Resources get allocated to one or the other — think of it as a budget:
  - Spend on growth (GH/IGF1 high) → fast development, reproduction, but less invested in repair and defense, and — it turns out — faster aging and shorter life.
  - Spend on maintenance (GH/IGF1 low) → slower growth, smaller body, but more antioxidants, more repair, more stress resistance, and slower aging, longer life.

## Key ideas

- This isn't hand-waving — one of the most reproducible findings in the field:
  - Dwarf mice (Ames, Snell, GH-receptor knockouts) have a broken GH/IGF1 axis. They're small — and live dramatically longer than normal mice.
  - People with Laron syndrome (no working GH receptor) are short-statured and appear strikingly protected from cancer and diabetes.
  - Dietary restriction (eating ~30% less) lowers GH/IGF1 signaling — and extends lifespan across yeast, worms, flies, mice, and likely primates.
- **Survival response**: repair-deficient mice are drowning in DNA damage. In response, they spontaneously turn the GH/IGF1 dial DOWN — which is why they're small and dwarfish. Their bodies have shifted budget away from growth toward protection. They're already, on their own, doing the exact thing that makes dwarf mice and DR animals long-lived — a built-in defensive reflex against the damage.
- **Dietary restriction works**: since the mice are already partially pushing this protective lever, what if we push harder on purpose? DR is a clean way to lower GH/IGF1 even further — and it works spectacularly: aging delayed in every organ, neurons most of all.
- **COMPENSATION / AGING HUB**: faced with overwhelming, unrepairable DNA damage, these short-lived repair mutants flip a metabolic switch. They downshift the GH/IGF1 axis and ramp up maintenance and defense (antioxidants, stress-resistance, quality-control machinery) — reallocating budget from "grow and reproduce" toward "survive and protect."
- **One gene mutated → MULTI-MORBIDITY.**
- The IGF gene is a sensor for aging.

## Background — the two repair pathways

- **Transcription-coupled repair (TCR)** specifically fixes damage on genes actively being transcribed — the strand the cell is currently using to make proteins. When TCR is broken, damage accumulates on actively transcribed genes, stalling transcription in non-dividing cells (neurons, liver, kidney). Those mice show accelerated aging — mirroring human diseases like **Cockayne Syndrome** and **Trichothiodystrophy (TTD)**, where patients age prematurely.
- **Global genome repair (GG-NER)** scans the entire genome for damage, including inactive parts. When broken, damage accumulates genome-wide → more mutations and cancer rather than dramatic aging (resembling **xeroderma pigmentosum**, the cancer-prone NER disease).
- **Crossing** = the genetics term for breeding two mouse strains together to combine mutations in offspring. They bred a TCR-deficient mouse with a global-genome-deficient mouse to get offspring lacking both pathways. The striking result is **synergy** — the combined effect is far worse than adding the two; the double mutant shows dramatically accelerated aging.
- **The Ercc1 hypomorph** (`-/Δ`, not a true `-/-` knockout):
  - One allele is a full null (`-`), completely non-functional.
  - The other (`Δ`) is a truncated Ercc1 (missing a small C-terminal piece) that still makes a partially working protein — a reduced-function, **hypomorphic** allele.
  - A complete `-/-` knockout is devastating: runted, dead within ~3–4 weeks of birth — too sick, too fast, to be an aging model. Keeping the partial `Δ` allele dials repair capacity down without switching it off: the animal develops virtually normally, then undergoes progressive, accelerated aging across essentially every organ system (neurodegeneration, sarcopenia, osteoporosis, kyphosis, vascular stiffness, immunosenescence, etc.).
