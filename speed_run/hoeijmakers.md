The key biological trade-off
Here's the central idea in all of aging biology that you need: a cell/body can't maximally do both "grow" and "protect/maintain" at the same time. Resources get allocated to one or the other. Think of it as a budget:

Spend on growth (GH/IGF1 high) → fast development, reproduction, but less invested in repair and defense, and — it turns out — faster aging and shorter life.
Spend on maintenance (GH/IGF1 low) → slower growth, smaller body, but more antioxidants, more repair, more stress resistance, and slower aging, longer life.

This isn't hand-waving. It's one of the most reproducible findings in the field:

Dwarf mice (Ames, Snell, GH-receptor knockouts) have a broken GH/IGF1 axis. They're small — and they live dramatically longer than normal mice.
People with Laron syndrome (no working GH receptor) are short-statured and appear strikingly protected from cancer and diabetes.
Dietary restriction (eating ~30% less) lowers GH/IGF1 signaling — and extends lifespan across yeast, worms, flies, mice, and likely primates.

Slide 3 ("survival response"): The repair-deficient mice are drowning in DNA damage. In response, they spontaneously turn the GH/IGF1 dial DOWN. That's why they're small and dwarfish — their bodies have shifted budget away from growth toward protection (antioxidants, stress resistance). The point of the slide was: the mice are already, on their own, doing the exact thing that makes dwarf mice and DR animals long-lived. It's a built-in defensive reflex against the damage.
Slide 4 (dietary restriction works): So Hoeijmakers asks the obvious next question — if the mice are already partially pushing this protective lever themselves, what if we push it harder, on purpose? DR is a clean way to lower GH/IGF1 even further. And it works spectacularly: aging delayed in every organ, neurons most of all.

Transcription-coupled repair (TCR) specifically fixes damage on genes that are actively being read (transcribed). It targets the DNA strand the cell is currently using to make proteins.

Global genome repair (GG-NER) scans the entire genome for damage, including the inactive parts that aren't being transcribed at the moment.

When TCR is broken, damage accumulates on actively transcribed genes. This stalls transcription in cells that aren't dividing (neurons, liver, kidney). Those mice show accelerated aging — and this mirrors real human diseases like Cockayne Syndrome and Trichothiodystrophy (TTD), where patients age prematurely.
When global genome repair is broken, damage accumulates genome-wide, leading to more mutations and cancer rather than dramatic aging. (This pattern resembles xeroderma pigmentosum, the cancer-prone NER disease.)

"Crossing" is the genetics term for breeding two mouse strains together to combine their mutations in the offspring. So they took a TCR-deficient mouse and a global-genome-deficient mouse and bred them to get offspring lacking both pathways.
The striking result is synergy — the combined effect is far worse than just adding the two together. A mouse missing both pathways shows dramatically accelerated aging, much more severe than either single mutant.

A true knockout would be written -/-: both copies of the gene completely dead. But this mouse is -/Δ:

One allele is a full null (-) — completely non-functional.
The other allele (Δ) is not dead. It's a truncated version of Ercc1 (missing a small piece at the C-terminus) that still makes a partially working protein.

So the animal has reduced but non-zero ERCC1 function. The term for a reduced-function allele like this is hypomorphic (a hypomorph)

Here's the punchline: a complete Ercc1 knockout (-/-) is devastating. Those mice are runted and die within about 3–4 weeks of birth — too sick, too fast, to be useful as an aging model. You can't study "progressive aging" in an animal that dies as a weanling.
By keeping that one partially functional Δ allele, you dial the repair capacity down without switching it off. The result is the animal on this slide: it develops virtually normally, then undergoes progressive, accelerated aging across essentially every organ system — the whole catalogue of phenotypes radiating off the mouse (neurodegeneration, sarcopenia, osteoporosis, kyphosis, vascular stiffness, immunosenescence, etc.).

Faced with overwhelming, unrepairable DNA damage piling up in their cells, these short-lived repair mutants flip a metabolic switch. They downshift the GH/IGF1 axis — the growth hormone / insulin-like growth factor signaling that normally drives the body to grow, build tissue, and reproduce.
In trade, they ramp up maintenance and defense: antioxidant systems, stress-resistance pathways, cellular repair and quality-control machinery. The body is essentially reallocating its budget from "grow and reproduce" toward "survive and protect."
